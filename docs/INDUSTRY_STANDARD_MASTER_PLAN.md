# Cricket Edge Industry-Standard Master Plan

Generated: 2026-07-13  
Status: canonical forward plan following the full-repository audit  
Primary focus: statistical modelling, data science, market evaluation, and betting research  
Execution boundary: paper-only; no real-money connector is in scope

## 1. Executive Verdict

Cricket Edge is a credible research prototype with several unusually good foundations for a solo sports-betting project: raw odds retention, typed provider clients, retries and timeouts, chronological team features, Elo and logistic baselines, probability calibration, no-vig market math, paper-only execution, explicit stale/demo odds gates, model-registry concepts, CLV plumbing, an audit trail, and 79 passing tests.

It is not yet an industry-grade betting research platform, and the current results must not be presented as evidence of a profitable edge. The main limitation is not the absence of a more sophisticated algorithm. It is the absence of a single reproducible, point-in-time-correct research dataset that connects historical cricket state, the exact information available at a decision timestamp, complete bookmaker markets, model artifacts, decisions, and later outcomes.

The most urgent issue is fail-closed safety. When a trained live model is unavailable, real fixtures currently fall back to deterministic demo/hash features. Those predictions can still see fresh real odds and pass the paper-betting gate. The current database demonstrates this: it contains zero model runs and two open paper bets generated by `baseline_t20_elo_market_v1`, including an ODI fixture stored as T20. This must be fixed before any new modelling work.

The professional target is not “add LightGBM” or “show a high accuracy number.” The target is an auditable research loop in which every prediction can answer:

1. What exact information was available at decision time?
2. Which immutable data snapshot, feature schema, code version, and model artifact produced the probability?
3. How did that probability compare with a market-only baseline and the incumbent model out of sample?
4. Was the quoted price complete, fresh, executable, and from the correct market and format?
5. What uncertainty and risk limits changed the stake or blocked the decision?
6. Did the price beat a consistently defined closing reference, and what happened after settlement?

## 4. Critical Findings and Priority

### P0 — Must be contained before further paper decisions

1. **Real-fixture model fallback is fail-open.** A missing trained model routes a real fixture through deterministic demo/hash features. Fresh real odds can then make the row bettable.
2. **Format scope is not enforced.** Provider fixtures are hard-coded as T20, while configured feeds include ODI and Test cricket. An ODI paper bet is currently labelled T20 and was scored by the T20 fallback model.
3. **Multiway market completeness is not enforced.** Draw outcomes are ignored by parsers. This is invalid for genuine three-way markets, especially Tests, and corrupts no-vig probabilities.
4. **Backtest time boundaries are date-only.** `date(captured_at) <= date(match_date)` can admit prices captured after the match started on the same day. Closing selection can also include in-play or post-start prices.
5. **Demo reset is destructive.** `/api/reset-demo` deletes every fixture, odds row, prediction, paper bet, decision, and event, including real research state.
6. **Readiness can give false reassurance.** It checks the existence of tests rather than a test result, counts any rows rather than validating recency/completeness, and does not block on a missing active artifact.
7. **Paper placement is not transactional or idempotent.** The check-then-insert flow has no unique constraint protecting fixture/market duplication under concurrent calls.

### P1 — Invalidates professional model selection if left unresolved

1. The same fixed holdout is repeatedly reused for training decisions and promotion.
2. Promotion is based on a single test Brier comparison with no uncertainty, segment guardrails, or real-market evidence.
3. Training artifacts live inside JSON payloads without an immutable dataset hash, code version, schema version, or validation signature.
4. Historical feature rows use cumulative aggregates rather than true rolling/decayed windows.
5. Team ordering is not explicitly made symmetric, and the historical backtest evaluates only `team_a` selections.
6. “Confidence” is a heuristic transformation of distance from 0.5 and data count, not calibrated uncertainty.
7. Prediction edge uses expected return (`p * odds - 1`) while the backtest uses probability edge (`p_model - p_market`); two different quantities share one label.
8. The market layer does not yet define opening, decision, and close as complete pre-match snapshots with exact UTC cutoffs.

### P2 — Limits cricket signal and market realism

1. The parser stores match and innings aggregates but not deliveries, player participation, innings phases, or lineup state.
2. Legal-ball counting excludes wides but not no-balls; super-over innings can overwrite regulation totals; dismissal types are not normalized for team-wicket semantics.
3. Men’s/women’s, domestic/international, and multiple competition strengths are pooled into one broad model.
4. Venue/home logic is string matching and is rarely meaningful for real cricket venues.
5. Team aliases are a hard-coded map rather than governed entity-resolution data with review status.
6. Weather is not linked; using observed historical weather later would leak information unless the feature represents a forecast vintage available at decision time.
7. Cash-out P&L is a simplified theoretical hedge with no spread, commission, rejection, or bookmaker cash-out rules.
8. SQLite serves operational state, analytical history, model metadata, and concurrent scheduler/UI work without migrations or job leases; a journal file is currently present.

## 5. North-Star Product Definition

Cricket Edge should become a local-first, paper-only cricket betting research workstation for pre-match match-winner markets, initially restricted to T20 cricket. Its professional identity should be:

- a probability and market-research system, not a tip generator;
- a reproducible data-science project, not a dashboard wrapped around demo logic;
- conservative about uncertainty and market evidence;
- explicit about what it cannot know;
- point-in-time correct by construction;
- capable of replaying every research result from immutable inputs;
- honest about small samples and incapable of labelling synthetic prices as proof of edge.

The initial supported strategy should be deliberately narrow:

- format: T20 only;
- timing: scheduled pre-match decision checkpoints, with a separate post-toss research model only when toss/lineup data is available;
- market: match winner, complete two-way markets only;
- execution: paper only;
- evaluation: market-only baseline, Elo, calibrated logistic, and challenger models;
- settlement: confirmed result and explicit void rules;
- performance standard: calibration and CLV before ROI claims.

ODI, Test, in-play, player props, totals, and real-money execution should be separate future products with separate data contracts and models.

## 8. Statistical Modelling and Data Science Programme

This is the core of the project. Work should proceed in the following order: define the prediction problem, guarantee point-in-time data, establish market and simple model baselines, build rolling evaluation, improve signal, then compare model families.

### 8.1 Prediction products and labels

Create explicit model products rather than one generic “cricket model”:

| Product | Decision time | Allowed inputs | Initial status |
|---|---|---|---|
| `t20_prematch_v1` | e.g. T-24h/T-6h/T-60m | history, venue, scheduled XI only if published, market available by cutoff | Primary |
| `t20_posttoss_v1` | after toss, before first ball | pre-match inputs plus toss and confirmed XI | Research/shadow |
| `odi_prematch_*` | ODI-specific | separate format data and model | Blocked/future |
| `test_prematch_*` | Test-specific, draw-aware | three-way label and market | Blocked/future |
| `t20_inplay_*` | after first ball | live score state and live prices | Blocked/future |

For the primary binary T20 product:

- label is the official match-winner settlement outcome under a documented market rule;
- ties/no-results/abandonments are void/excluded according to the target bookmaker rule, not silently coded as a loss;
- super-over winners are handled consistently;
- each row has `decision_at_utc`, `scheduled_start_at_utc`, `information_cutoff_at_utc`, and `result_known_at_utc`;
- each prediction is made for both mutually exclusive selections and must sum to one before market comparison;
- swapping team A/B must produce complementary probabilities within a strict tolerance.

### 8.2 Canonical dataset contract

Implement one typed `DatasetSpec`/`FeatureSpec` used by both historical and live paths. Each feature definition must include:

- name and version;
- semantic meaning and unit;
- model timing (`pre_toss`, `post_toss`, future `in_play`);
- entity grain;
- source tables/columns;
- event time and ingestion time;
- lookback window and decay;
- availability rule at decision time;
- missing/default policy;
- coverage indicator;
- transformation and expected range;
- leakage test;
- live implementation reference.

Every training row should carry:

- `dataset_version` and content hash;
- raw snapshot hashes;
- feature-schema version;
- decision policy/version;
- maximum source event time used;
- model-eligible reason or exclusion reason;
- entity-resolution version;
- label/settlement-rule version.

Fail the build on duplicate match IDs, impossible chronology, unresolved labels, post-cutoff inputs, non-complementary labels, unsupported formats, or missing mandatory coverage flags.

### 8.3 Entity resolution and reference data

Replace hard-coded aliases with canonical dimension tables:

- `teams` and `team_aliases` with canonical ID, provider, validity interval, format/competition context, match method, confidence, reviewer, and review timestamp;
- `competitions` with format, gender, domestic/international tier, country, season, and strength group;
- `venues` with canonical ID, city, country, timezone, coordinates, altitude where useful, and known names;
- `players` and `player_aliases` when player features are added;
- `provider_events` with stable provider event ID and canonical fixture ID.

Entity matching should be deterministic first, fuzzy only as a review suggestion. Ambiguous links remain quarantined. Report unmatched and multiply matched rates by provider. No paper decision is eligible until both teams, format, market, and event time resolve unambiguously.

### 8.4 Cricsheet parsing and cricket data correctness

Before building new features, add golden fixtures for difficult match cases:

- wides and no-balls in legal-ball counts;
- retired hurt, retired out, obstructing the field, and other dismissal semantics;
- super overs without overwriting regulation innings aggregates;
- DLS/revised targets;
- ties, no-results, abandoned matches, and forfeits;
- multiple innings and non-T20 files rejected from the T20 dataset;
- women’s/domestic/international competition metadata;
- renamed/franchise teams and neutral venues.

Persist normalized deliveries rather than only innings totals. Minimum delivery fields should include innings, over, ball index, striker, non-striker, bowler, batting team, runs off bat, extras by type, total runs, wicket kind, dismissed player, fielders, target/revised target metadata, and source event time/version.

Reconcile every ingest:

- raw files seen = parsed + quarantined;
- match totals = sum of delivery totals under documented rules;
- innings and delivery counts match source metadata;
- no orphan innings/deliveries;
- schema/version drift creates an alert, not silent skipping.

### 8.5 Feature-store roadmap

Features should be added in audited groups and each group must earn its place through rolling ablation.

#### Group A — strong permanent baselines

- format-specific Elo/Glicko-style team strength;
- separate batting and bowling ratings;
- opponent-adjusted results;
- time decay and inactivity uncertainty;
- domestic/international and gender hierarchy;
- promoted/relegated or new-team priors with shrinkage.

#### Group B — recent team form

- last 3/5/10 and exponentially weighted results;
- runs per legal ball, wickets lost per innings, wickets taken, economy proxy;
- powerplay/middle/death run and wicket rates;
- batting-first versus chasing splits;
- opponent-strength-adjusted net run-rate proxy;
- rest days, travel/timezone if reliable, and schedule congestion;
- effective sample size for every rolling estimate.

#### Group C — venue and conditions

- venue first-innings par distribution by era/competition;
- chase advantage and toss interaction;
- pace/spin or phase scoring profiles when derivable;
- home/neutral designation from canonical country/venue data;
- team venue/country experience with shrinkage;
- season and time-of-year effects.

Observed historical weather must not be treated as though it were known before a match. A pre-match weather feature requires the forecast vintage that was available at the decision timestamp. Until forecast vintages and venue coordinates exist, weather remains excluded from production features.

#### Group D — player and lineup strength

- confirmed XI availability and source timestamp;
- player batting/bowling ratings with recency and competition adjustment;
- expected contribution and replacement-level priors;
- lineup continuity, role balance, handedness/bowling style where reliable;
- injuries/availability only from structured timestamped sources;
- uncertainty when XI is projected rather than confirmed.

Build the interface before claiming player signal; do not fabricate lineups.

#### Group E — market features available at decision time

- no-vig consensus probability;
- best executable price and reference-book price kept separately;
- bookmaker dispersion;
- opening-to-decision movement;
- time to start and quote age;
- overround/liquidity proxies;
- provider/bookmaker coverage count.

The closing line is an evaluation label only and never a training feature for a pre-close decision.

### 8.6 Baseline discipline and model families

Maintain a clear ladder:

1. Unconditional and segment base rates.
2. Market-only no-vig probability at the decision timestamp.
3. Format-specific Elo.
4. Calibrated regularized logistic regression on cricket features.
5. Market-offset logistic model:

   `logit(p_model) = logit(p_market_at_decision) + f(cricket_features)`

   This asks whether cricket information adds value beyond the market and naturally shrinks implausibly large edges.
6. Regularized interactions/generalized additive model where nonlinear shape is interpretable.
7. One production-grade boosted-tree challenger (LightGBM or XGBoost) after the evaluation harness is trusted.
8. A conservative ensemble/stack only if out-of-fold predictions prove incremental value.

Do not add many model families at once. Each candidate requires a pre-registered hypothesis, bounded search space, rolling out-of-fold predictions, calibration, artifact support, and an ablation report. Elo and logistic remain permanent reference models.

Potential longer-term research includes hierarchical Bayesian partial pooling for teams/competitions and dynamic ratings with uncertainty. This is more valuable than a deep neural network at the current data scale. Neural models are out of scope until ball/player data volume, validation, and market evidence justify them.

### 8.7 Rolling-origin validation design

Replace the fixed 70/15/15 selection loop with nested rolling-origin evaluation:

- choose calendar-duration folds rather than arbitrary row percentages;
- train only on matches strictly before the calibration window;
- optionally insert a time gap if source finalization can lag;
- fit calibration on a forward window disjoint from training;
- evaluate on the next untouched window;
- roll forward through at least five folds where data permits;
- preserve a final audit period that is never used for feature or hyperparameter decisions;
- evaluate the actual scheduled retraining policy, not just a static model;
- store every out-of-fold prediction immutably.

Hyperparameter optimization must be nested inside the training portion of each outer fold. It must never see outer test or final audit results. Repeated daily retraining must not repeatedly promote against the same test set.

Fold reports should include:

- exact train/calibration/test dates and row counts;
- competition, gender, format, team, and era coverage;
- Brier, log loss, calibration intercept/slope, ECE, and Brier decomposition;
- market-only and incumbent paired deltas;
- bootstrap confidence intervals clustered by match date/competition where appropriate;
- segment metrics only when the segment has a declared minimum sample;
- real-price betting outcomes only for rows with valid as-of markets.

### 8.8 Calibration and uncertainty

Move calibration into a reusable module supporting sigmoid/Platt, isotonic with minimum sample protection, and temperature scaling where applicable. The calibrator always fits on predictions from data not used to fit the base model.

Report before/after:

- Brier and log loss;
- reliability diagram with bin counts and confidence intervals;
- calibration intercept and slope;
- ECE plus maximum calibration error;
- sharpness/resolution and Brier decomposition;
- segment and recent-window calibration.

Replace the current “confidence” score with explicit components:

- predictive probability and calibrated interval;
- data coverage/effective sample size;
- out-of-distribution score versus training features;
- alias/entity-resolution status;
- model age and training cutoff;
- calibration-bin support;
- market completeness, freshness, and dispersion;
- ensemble or bootstrap disagreement if available.

The risk engine should use an uncertainty-adjusted probability, such as a conservative lower bound or probability shrunk toward the market/base rate. Distance from 0.5 is not itself confidence.

### 8.9 Statistical inference and experiment governance

For every candidate:

- record the hypothesis before running the final comparison;
- publish paired score differences against incumbent and market baseline;
- use block/cluster bootstrap intervals, not only point estimates;
- show fold dispersion and worst-fold result;
- control model-search degrees of freedom and record every attempted run;
- distinguish exploratory, candidate, shadow, active-prediction, and paper-enabled states;
- label small or biased samples as inconclusive;
- never select on ROI alone.

Profit and CLV estimates need uncertainty intervals. A positive point estimate with an interval spanning meaningful loss is not evidence of edge.

### 8.10 Model artifact and registry contract

Each immutable artifact must include:

- unique run/artifact ID and semantic model name;
- training code Git SHA and dirty-worktree flag;
- Python/dependency lock hash;
- dataset, raw-snapshot, feature-schema, entity-map, and label-policy hashes;
- model family/hyperparameters/random seeds;
- preprocessing/scaler and feature order;
- fitted model parameters;
- calibrator parameters;
- fold definitions and out-of-fold metrics;
- market/backtest evidence IDs;
- supported format, market, timing, competitions, and validity rules;
- creation/training cutoff and expiry/review date;
- promotion verdict, approver/mode, and rollback parent.

Artifact load must validate the schema and fail closed. A round-trip test must produce identical probabilities within numerical tolerance. Live prediction loads the registry’s validated active artifact only; it never loads “latest run” implicitly.

### 8.11 Champion/challenger lifecycle

Use distinct gates:

- `exploratory`: may be analysed only;
- `candidate`: passed data and rolling-evaluation checks;
- `shadow`: produces immutable predictions but cannot place paper bets;
- `active_prediction`: may populate dashboard probabilities;
- `paper_enabled`: may enter the risk gate after market and sample requirements pass;
- `retired`/`blocked`: preserved for audit, never loaded.

Promotion should require paired improvement or clear non-inferiority in proper scoring rules, acceptable calibration, no material high-volume segment regression, valid artifact lineage, and sufficient market evidence. Insufficient odds evidence returns `inconclusive`, never automatic promotion.

## 9. Odds and Market-Intelligence Plan

### 9.1 Canonical market snapshot

A quote must be stored as part of a complete market state, not merely a selection/price row. Required fields include:

- canonical fixture and provider event IDs;
- bookmaker/exchange key and region;
- market and period;
- market rules/version and number of expected outcomes;
- selection canonical ID;
- back/lay side where relevant;
- decimal odds;
- provider `last_update_at`, received time, and persisted time in UTC;
- scheduled start time in UTC;
- pre-match/live/suspended/closed state;
- quote group/snapshot ID shared by all outcomes;
- expected and observed outcome count;
- overround and no-vig method;
- mapping confidence and entity-resolution version;
- raw payload hash/path;
- ingestion run and parser version.

Only complete, mutually exclusive markets are eligible for no-vig calculation or betting. A Test match with a draw is a three-way market; dropping the draw is prohibited. Unsupported market shapes are quarantined.

### 9.2 Price definitions

Define these once and use them everywhere:

- **Opening price:** first complete eligible pre-match snapshot for a bookmaker/reference market.
- **Decision price:** latest complete executable snapshot at or before the declared decision timestamp.
- **Best price:** best eligible price available across approved bookmakers at decision time.
- **Reference price:** price from the chosen reference bookmaker or robust consensus used for comparison.
- **Closing price:** last complete pre-match reference snapshot strictly before the scheduled start, with a defined safety buffer; never an in-play/post-start quote.

Keep bookmaker-specific and consensus prices separate. Do not create an overround from selections captured at different times or different bookmakers.

### 9.3 Market probability and edge definitions

Use separate names and units:

- raw implied probability: `1 / decimal_odds`;
- no-vig market probability: margin-removed probability for a complete market;
- probability edge: `p_model - p_market_no_vig`;
- expected return/EV: `p_model * decimal_odds - 1`;
- conservative EV: `p_conservative * decimal_odds - 1`;
- odds CLV: `entry_decimal / closing_decimal - 1`;
- probability CLV: decision/closing no-vig probability movement, with direction defined explicitly;
- log-price CLV: `log(entry_decimal / closing_decimal)` for additive aggregation.

Multiplicative normalization is the permanent simple no-vig baseline. Later compare additive/power/Shin-style methods where market shape and favourite-longshot bias justify them. The chosen method is versioned and tested.

### 9.4 Collection strategy

Build an append-only collector with quota-aware schedules and job metrics. A useful target cadence, adjusted for provider limits, is:

- T-48h to T-24h: every 2-4 hours;
- T-24h to T-6h: hourly;
- T-6h to T-60m: every 15 minutes;
- T-60m to T-10m: every 5 minutes;
- final pre-start window: every 1-2 minutes only if quota and terms permit.

Persist every raw response and every parsing failure. Track events requested, complete markets, incomplete markets, changed quotes, unchanged responses, rate-limit cost, latency, retries, and errors. Backoff must not silently create a “closing” label from an old quote.

The repository already contains 591 raw odds files but only 24 structured current rows. Add a raw replay command and reconciliation report so a database reset never discards the research asset.

If budget permits, a paid historical endpoint can accelerate evidence collection. The Odds API documents historical snapshots from June 2020 and five-minute snapshots from September 2022 on paid plans. Provider coverage for cricket/bookmakers must be sampled before purchase; never assume the nominal date range gives sufficient cricket depth.

### 9.5 Market-quality features and controls

- separate bookmaker best price, median consensus, and reference/sharper price;
- record overround and exclude extreme/invalid books;
- measure staleness per quote, not only per provider;
- flag one-sided/incomplete markets;
- detect price spikes, reversed teams, and stale provider timestamps;
- track line movement, disagreement, and quote count;
- store commission and exchange adjustments;
- preserve bookmaker rules for ties, voids, and dead heats;
- add manual mapping/review queue for unmatched events;
- monitor closing coverage by competition and provider.

## 10. Backtesting Engine V2

The target is an event-driven simulator that reuses live feature, decision, risk, and settlement functions.

### 10.1 Simulation clock

Process in strict chronological order:

1. New raw data becomes available.
2. Canonical state is updated.
3. The scheduled decision checkpoint fires.
4. Features are built using an as-of cutoff.
5. The versioned model generates both selection probabilities.
6. Complete market state is selected as of the cutoff.
7. Risk rules produce a bet or explicit skip.
8. The paper order records requested and accepted price assumptions.
9. Later quotes update CLV only; they cannot alter the decision.
10. Settlement updates bankroll after the official result becomes known.

Use full UTC timestamps. Date-only comparisons are prohibited in research eligibility.

### 10.2 Candidate generation

- generate both sides of each binary market;
- use the same edge/EV definitions as live code;
- allow at most one net position per fixture/market/decision policy;
- prevent selecting both opposing outcomes unless explicitly simulating an arbitrage/hedge strategy;
- preserve every skip reason to expose selection bias;
- distinguish model candidate count, market-valid count, risk-valid count, requested bets, and filled bets;
- exclude demo, synthetic, incomplete, post-start, unsupported-format, and low-confidence rows.

### 10.3 Execution realism

Support configurable scenarios:

- flat stake;
- fixed percentage of current bankroll;
- fractional Kelly;
- uncertainty-adjusted/capped Kelly;
- bookmaker/exchange commission;
- price slippage and decision latency;
- maximum accepted stake/partial fill;
- rejection probability in stress scenarios;
- minimum/maximum odds;
- stale quote and market suspension;
- void/dead-heat rules;
- cash-out/hedge as a separately labelled strategy.

Do not mix simulated cash-out P&L into the main pre-match strategy result until the hedge price and cost model is defensible.

### 10.4 Portfolio simulation

Bankroll, open exposure, and limits evolve through time. Enforce:

- per-bet, fixture, team, competition, format, model, bookmaker, and day caps;
- correlated exposure for the same team/tournament/time window;
- daily stop and drawdown throttles;
- no staking with unsettled funds beyond configured availability;
- idempotent order keys;
- chronological settlement before later staking decisions where applicable.

### 10.5 Reports

Every run must report:

- candidates, eligible markets, bets, skips by reason, wins/losses/voids;
- starting/ending bankroll, turnover, P&L, ROI/yield;
- equity curve, max drawdown, drawdown duration/time under water;
- average/max exposure and concurrent positions;
- hit rate, average odds, average model probability, average EV;
- odds/probability/log-price CLV and positive-CLV rate;
- performance by fold, month, competition, gender, team-history coverage, bookmaker, price band, edge bucket, uncertainty bucket, and model version;
- block-bootstrap intervals for ROI, CLV, and model-score deltas;
- sensitivity to thresholds, slippage, commission, and probability shrinkage;
- comparison with market-only, Elo, incumbent, and “no bet” baselines.

### 10.6 Backtest anti-bias checks

- point-in-time/as-of joins are unit and property tested;
- feature maximum timestamp never exceeds decision timestamp;
- close is not used to choose a bet;
- incomplete and missing markets remain missing rather than imputed as neutral;
- delisted/failed providers remain in the historical record;
- competition filtering is defined before results are seen;
- repeated hyperparameter trials are logged;
- the final audit period remains untouched;
- team-order swap tests prove complementarity;
- replaying the same run produces the same order ledger and metrics.

## 11. Staking and Risk Management Plan

The system should behave like a conservative risk desk around uncertain estimates.

### 11.1 Probability and edge controls

- require a valid `paper_enabled` artifact for the exact format/market/timing;
- require complete fresh real odds and a supported settlement rule;
- shrink probability toward the market or base rate based on uncertainty;
- stake from conservative EV, not raw point probability;
- block unusually large edges as possible data/mapping errors pending review;
- block out-of-distribution feature rows and thin calibration buckets;
- use price and probability edges as separate policy inputs;
- record every component of the decision.

### 11.2 Suggested initial paper limits

These are governance starting points, not universal industry rules, and must be stress-tested:

- Kelly fraction: at most 0.10-0.25 after probability shrinkage;
- maximum stake: 0.25-0.50% of current bankroll until evidence matures;
- fixture/market cap: one net position;
- daily open exposure: 2-3%;
- competition exposure: 1-1.5%;
- same-team daily exposure: 1%;
- drawdown throttle: halve stakes after 5% peak-to-trough paper drawdown;
- stop new orders after 10% drawdown pending review;
- no paper stake below the configured meaningful minimum;
- no betting when data/model/odds health is degraded.

Run Monte Carlo/bootstrap bankroll paths using the observed price and outcome distribution to estimate risk of ruin, drawdown quantiles, longest losing streak, and time to recovery. Fractional Kelly is not safe if the probability estimate is biased.

### 11.3 Risk engine architecture

Return a structured verdict with independent gates:

- data eligibility;
- model eligibility;
- market eligibility;
- statistical uncertainty;
- positive conservative EV;
- order/duplicate control;
- bankroll availability;
- correlated portfolio exposure;
- daily loss/drawdown state;
- operational health.

The final stake should show raw Kelly, uncertainty haircut, cap adjustments, and final value. A veto is immutable and audit-linked to the proposal.

## 18. Proposed Quantitative Governance Gates

These are project governance targets, not universal industry standards. Revise them only through a recorded decision.

### Gate A — Data eligible

- 100% of model rows have supported format/market/timing and valid UTC chronology.
- 100% of prediction features have `max_source_time <= decision_at`.
- Raw-to-parsed reconciliation is complete; quarantines are explicit.
- Duplicate canonical fixtures/markets are zero.
- Complete-market rate is at least 99% for paper-eligible rows.
- Team/event mapping is deterministic or manually approved; ambiguous rows are zero in eligible data.
- Label rules pass all golden cricket cases.

### Gate B — Model candidate

- At least five rolling outer folds where the history permits, with declared calendar windows.
- Each headline fold/segment metric shows its sample size; major segment guardrails require at least 100 rows.
- Candidate improves or is non-inferior to incumbent on paired out-of-fold log loss/Brier and beats the market-only baseline on a declared criterion.
- Improvement is stable across most folds and no major segment has a material unexplained regression.
- Calibration slope target 0.90-1.10, intercept approximately -0.05 to 0.05, and ECE target no worse than 0.03 where sample/bin counts make that meaningful.
- Artifact round-trip probability difference is below `1e-12` for deterministic models.
- Final audit period has not been used for feature/hyperparameter choice.

### Gate C — Shadow active

- Artifact, dataset, feature, entity-map, and code lineage validate.
- Scope match is exact and unsupported formats fail closed.
- At least 30 consecutive days of reliable shadow predictions or an equivalent representative replay.
- No lookahead, duplicate, or reconciliation failure.
- Missing/OOD and alias rates are within declared thresholds.
- Calibration and score drift are monitored as results arrive.

### Gate D — Paper enabled

- At least 300 resolved timestamp-valid paper/backtest bets is a preferred initial evidence floor, with sample composition reported.
- At least 80% have a consistently defined pre-start closing reference; missing closes are analysed for bias.
- Mean CLV is positive and its block-bootstrap interval is reported; a lower bound above zero is the stronger target.
- ROI/yield, drawdown, and risk-of-ruin scenarios are acceptable under slippage/commission stress; ROI alone cannot pass the gate.
- No competition/bookmaker/edge bucket dominates the result without explicit concentration analysis.
- Risk, order idempotency, settlement, backup, and reconciliation tests all pass.

Until Gate D passes, challengers may remain prediction/shadow models and the system remains a research workstation.

## 23. Definition of Done for the Overall Programme

The project can be described as an experienced, professional betting research system when:

## 25. Final Recommendation

Treat Cricket Edge as a research platform whose product is trustworthy evidence. The best next investment is not model complexity. It is the SAFE-01 to SAFE-06 containment work followed by a replayable, exact-timestamp, market-aware dataset. Once that foundation exists, the project’s current Elo/logistic/calibration work can become a genuinely strong baseline and richer cricket features can be judged honestly.

The mark of an experienced bettor is not the size of a claimed edge. It is disciplined price definitions, conservative probability estimates, ruthless avoidance of leakage, respect for market baselines, control of bankroll risk, and willingness to say “no bet” or “insufficient evidence.” This plan is designed to make those qualities visible in both the code and the results.
